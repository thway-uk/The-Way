Samuel Summary: Neville Goddard Style

The biblical story of Samuel, Saul, and David offers a rich narrative of transformation, self-discovery, and manifestation. Through the teachings of Neville Goddard, these figures serve as symbols for different stages in our journey of awakening and creative expression. Each character embodies a stage of consciousness, from the initial stirring of awareness to the ultimate manifestation of divine potential.

Samuel: The Awakening to the "I AM"

Samuel means "God has heard" (שְׁמוּאֵל, Shemuel), but Neville Goddard’s interpretation carries a deeper significance. Samuel represents the first awareness of the "I AM" within us—the divine spark that awakens us to the realisation that we are more than our outer circumstances. In the Bible, when Samuel hears God’s voice calling him in the night, he initially mistakes it for Eli. This illustrates how, at the beginning of our spiritual journey, we may fail to recognise the voice of our own "I AM"—the divine calling within.

However, once Samuel listens and responds with “Speak, for your servant is listening,” he aligns himself with the divine within. This marks the point of awakening—the moment when we realise that we are not merely passive observers of life, but active creators of our reality. Samuel’s name, which signifies "hearing," is symbolic of the beginning of the process where we start to hear the divine voice within ourselves, guiding us toward our true potential.

Bible Verse: “Speak, for your servant is listening.” (1 Samuel 3:10)
This response is the epitome of the first step in Neville Goddard's process of manifestation: recognising the inner voice of the "I AM" and being open to its guidance.

Saul: The Ego and the Resistance to Change

Saul means “asked for” or “requested” (שָׁאוּל, Sha'ul), reflecting the desire for something external—a king chosen by the people, not by divine will. Saul embodies the ego, the part of us that resists change and insists on relying on external validation. His reign represents the phase of the old self—the mind caught in the illusion of external approval, fear, and doubt.

Saul was anointed king but chose to rely on his understanding of the world rather than divine guidance. His disobedience to God’s command (1 Samuel 15:22) is a clear example of how the ego often clings to familiar patterns of thinking that are no longer useful. Saul’s downfall shows us the consequences of ignoring the inner voice of the "I AM" in favour of external pressures and fears. His failure to listen to God ultimately leads to his rejection as king.

Saul’s story highlights the battle between the old self—the ego—and the higher consciousness that is calling us to let go of outdated beliefs and align with the truth of our creative power. This internal conflict mirrors our own resistance to embracing the new self, which is aligned with divine will.

Bible Verse: “To obey is better than sacrifice.” (1 Samuel 15:22)
This verse underscores the importance of aligning our actions with the guidance of the "I AM," rather than being swayed by external factors.

Saul vs. Cain: A More Evolved State of Consciousness

In comparison to Cain, Saul represents a more evolved state of awareness. While Cain, in his jealousy and fear, represents the outer man who is disconnected from divine guidance, Saul has been chosen for a purpose—he is more evolved, but still not fully aligned with the "I AM." Saul’s rejection of divine instruction parallels the internal struggle we face when we are torn between our old ways of thinking and the awakening to a higher self. While Saul has a more developed awareness than Cain, his ego still binds him to external outcomes, preventing him from fully realising his divine potential.

David: The Manifestation of the Divine Self

David means "beloved" (דָּוִד, Dawid), signifying one who is deeply loved by God. In Neville Goddard’s teachings, David represents the fully realised self—the embodiment of divine purpose and the creative power of the "I AM." David’s anointing by Samuel (1 Samuel 16:13) is the moment when the divine potential within is recognised and empowered. Unlike Saul, who was chosen for external reasons, David is chosen for his heart—his inner alignment with the divine will.

David’s victory over Goliath (1 Samuel 17) is a perfect metaphor for the manifestation process. David does not rely on conventional weapons or physical strength but on his faith in the "I AM." This shows that true power lies in inner conviction and imagination, not in external appearances. Through his trust in his own inner power, David manifests victory over what appears to be an insurmountable obstacle.

Bible Verse: “You come against me with sword and spear, but I come against you in the name of the Lord Almighty.” (1 Samuel 17:45)
David’s words illustrate the strength that comes from the "I AM"—the inner conviction that, with divine guidance, we can overcome all external challenges.

Key Lessons from the Interactions of Samuel, Saul, and David

The interactions between these three figures offer key lessons in the process of manifestation:

  1. Samuel: The Awakening to the "I AM"
    Samuel represents the first step in the manifestation process—awakening to the voice of the "I AM" within. This is the recognition that we are more than our current reality and that we have the power to create our own experiences.

  2. Saul: The Resistance of the Ego
    Saul represents the ego, which resists change and clings to old beliefs. His downfall illustrates the importance of letting go of the past and aligning with the divine will. To manifest fully, we must relinquish our attachment to the external world and trust the guidance of the "I AM."

  3. David: The Realisation and Manifestation of Divine Purpose
    David embodies the fully realised self—one who has embraced the "I AM" and manifests their desires with confidence and clarity. His victory over Goliath serves as a powerful reminder that when we align with our true self, we can overcome any obstacle.

Conclusion: Embrace the Power of the "I AM"

The story of Samuel, Saul, and David is not just a tale from the past; it is a timeless allegory for our own journey of awakening and manifestation. Samuel teaches us to listen to the inner voice of the "I AM," Saul shows us the pitfalls of clinging to the ego and external validation, and David demonstrates the power of fully embracing our divine potential. By following the path of these figures, we can move from limitation to liberation, from external dependence to the realisation of our creative power.
