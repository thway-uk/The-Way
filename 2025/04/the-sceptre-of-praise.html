The Sceptre of Praise

Moved 

“The sceptre shall not depart from Judah...” —Genesis 49:10

At the heart of the Bible lies a profound psychological drama of consciousness. Judah’s sceptre is more than a symbol of royal power; it is the authority of the masculine principle within the soul — the inner “I Am” that wields the law of assumption to manifest reality.

Neville Goddard’s teaching illuminates this sceptre as the creative rod of dominion, sustained by the feeling of praise and the persistent assumption of the fulfilled desire.

Judah: The Embodiment of the Masculine ‘I Am’ and Creative Authority

Judah’s name means “He shall be praised,” signalling that his dominion is founded on praise — not passive worship but the active feeling of the wish fulfilled. The sceptre in Judah’s hand is the law of assumption in action: the masculine consciousness holding firm to its imagined reality, shaping the world from within.

The Male Narrative in the Song of Solomon as Judah’s Inner Story

The Song of Solomon is a poetic unfolding of the masculine principle’s journey — the very essence of Judah’s sceptre brought to life. The male figure in the Song symbolizes the creative ‘I Am’ consciousness, the holder of the sceptre and rod, which are metaphors for power, identity, and ruling authority.

Consider these verses:

  • Song of Solomon 3:9-10
    “King Solomon made himself a palanquin of the wood of Lebanon... its pillars of silver, its support of gold, its seat of purple.”
    This regal imagery mirrors the authority Judah’s sceptre represents — the kingly stature of the masculine ‘I Am’ consciousness.

  • Song of Solomon 6:9
    “Who is she that looketh forth as the morning, fair as the moon, clear as the sun, and terrible as an army with banners?”
    The “army with banners” evokes dominion and command, the outward expression of Judah’s sceptre’s inner power.

  • Song of Solomon 7:7-8
    “Thy stature is like to a palm tree...”
    The palm tree is a biblical symbol of victory and kingship, reinforcing the male’s victorious rulership.

  • Song of Solomon 8:6
    “Set me as a seal upon thine heart, as a seal upon thine arm...”
    The seal symbolizes identity and divine authority — the mark of the ‘I Am’ who assumes and manifests.

Tamar and the Feminine Soul: The Receptive Partner to Judah’s Masculine Authority

Tamar, Judah’s daughter-in-law, represents the soul’s receptive aspect—the feminine principle necessary to conceive and bring forth the creative reality assumed by the masculine sceptre. Together, Judah and Tamar portray the sacred union of masculine dominion and feminine receptivity, a dynamic essential for manifestation.

Praise as the Sustaining Force of the Sceptre’s Power

Judah’s sceptre remains only through the vibrational state of praise — the inner certainty that the desired reality is already complete. Neville taught that you must enter the state and remain faithful to it; praise is how this faithfulness is maintained.

Mary’s proclamation in Luke 1:46, “My soul doth magnify the Lord,” echoes this principle. To magnify the Lord within is to sustain the masculine creative power of the ‘I Am’ through praise, the very essence of the sceptre.

Conclusion: The Sceptre as the Male Creative Principle in the Soul

The sceptre in Judah’s hand is the law of assumption made manifest — the masculine narrative of the Song of Solomon embodied in spiritual authority and creative power. This sceptre is not a physical object but the inner rod of rulership, held by the ‘I Am’ consciousness that persists in praise and assumes the fulfilment of its desire.

To wield the sceptre is to embody the male principle of creation: knowing, assuming, and ruling your world from the unshakable feeling of “I Am.”
