Paul: The Bible’s Symbolic Teacher

The Bible has been interpreted in countless ways throughout history, but one of the most transformative approaches is understanding it through symbolism and allegory. For too long, many have approached the Bible with a literal mindset, missing out on the profound spiritual principles that lie beneath the surface. In fact, the Bible text itself suggests that its stories, characters, and events are not to be taken literally, but instead are symbolic representations of deeper spiritual truths.

The Veil of Literalism

In Galatians 4:24-26 (NIV), Paul explicitly states that the story of Sarah and Hagar is "an allegory," and in Romans 2:28-29 (NIV), he teaches that the true meaning of being a "Jew" is not external but internal, representing an inner spiritual awakening. The Bible, through these passages, invites us to look beyond the surface and understand that its characters and events represent internal states of consciousness. When we read the Bible literally, we blind ourselves to its true power—the transformative power of symbolism.

The very act of reading the Bible literally can be compared to wearing a veil over our eyes. The veil represents our attachment to the material world and external realities, preventing us from seeing the deeper principles embedded within. As Paul warns in 2 Corinthians 3:6 (NIV):

"the letter kills, but the Spirit gives life."

The literal letter leads to a limited understanding, while the symbolic, spiritual interpretation opens the door to life-changing wisdom.

Paul’s Teaching on Allegory

In Galatians 3:24 (KJV), Paul tells us that the law (and the stories associated with it) was our "schoolmaster" to bring us to Christ, implying that these stories serve as symbolic lessons guiding us to a higher understanding. Later, in Galatians 4:24-26 (ESV), he specifically refers to the story of Sarah and Hagar as an allegory, showing us that the characters and events in the Bible often represent spiritual principles rather than literal history.

Furthermore, in 1 Corinthians 10:11 (NASB), Paul says:

"all these things happened to them as examples, and they were written for our instruction."

Here, Paul affirms that the events of the Old Testament are "examples" meant to teach us lessons—not facts to be memorised, but symbols to be understood.

The Role of Parables and Allegories

Jesus Himself made frequent use of parables, symbolic stories that teach profound truths. In Matthew 13:10-11 (NIV), He tells His disciples that He speaks in parables because:

"it is given unto you to know the mysteries of the kingdom of heaven, but to them it is not given."

Parables are designed to veil the deeper meanings, only revealing them to those who are ready to understand. Jesus’ use of parables suggests that many of His teachings are symbolic, hidden beneath the surface, and only accessible to those who seek the inner meaning.

In fact, Jesus often emphasised that the external, literal interpretation of events and teachings was insufficient. In John 6:63 (NIV), He says:

"The Spirit gives life; the flesh counts for nothing. The words I have spoken to you—they are full of the Spirit and life."

Jesus’ words, like the Bible as a whole, are spiritual, not material. The literal flesh cannot provide us with the nourishment we need; it is the spirit that brings us life, and we must interpret His teachings with that in mind.

Beyond Rituals: A Spiritual Reformation

The Bible also teaches us that many of the rituals and customs prescribed in the Old Testament are mere "shadows" of something greater. In Colossians 2:16-17 (NIV), Paul writes:

"Therefore do not let anyone judge you by what you eat or drink, or with regard to a religious festival, a New Moon celebration or a Sabbath day. These are a shadow of the things that were to come; the reality, however, is found in Christ."

These outward rituals point to deeper spiritual truths, much like the physical laws in the Old Testament were symbolic representations of inner laws that govern spiritual awakening.

Hebrews 9:9-10 (NASB) further reinforces this idea, describing the Old Testament rituals as

"figures" for a time that was to come, a time of spiritual reformation.

These rites and observances were not meant to be followed literally, but symbolised the internal spiritual processes that lead to personal transformation.

The Power of Inner Transformation

One of the most powerful messages of the Bible is that transformation comes from within. In Romans 2:28-29 (NIV), Paul teaches that true circumcision is not of the flesh but of the heart, highlighting the importance of inner change over external observances. Similarly, in 2 Corinthians 3:6 (NASB), Paul contrasts the

"letter" of the law with the "spirit" that gives life.

External rituals and the literal understanding of scripture may provide some structure, but it is the internal transformation, the shift in consciousness, that is the key to spiritual growth and manifestation.

Jesus' teachings, particularly in the Gospel of John, urge us to focus on the spiritual essence of His message rather than the literal, material world. His words are

"spirit" and "life,"

showing us that the deeper meanings of the Bible are about awakening the spirit within.

The Allegory of Baptism

In 1 Peter 3:21 (NIV), Peter refers to baptism as

"the pledge of a clear conscience toward God,"

rather than a literal cleansing of the body. This further underscores the Bible’s emphasis on transformation over physical acts. Baptism, like all other rituals, is symbolic of a deeper spiritual rebirth, a reawakening of the mind and spirit.

The Leaven of the Pharisees

In Matthew 16:6 (NASB), Jesus warns His disciples to

"beware of the leaven of the Pharisees and of the Sadducees,"

using leaven as a metaphor for the corrupting influence of false teachings and mindsets. Just as leaven spreads through dough, so too do ideas spread through the mind. This passage reinforces the idea that the Bible uses metaphors to convey spiritual lessons about the influence of thoughts and beliefs on our lives.

Conclusion: Embrace the Power of Symbolism

The Bible is a treasure trove of wisdom, but only when we read it with an understanding that its stories are often allegorical, symbolic representations of deeper truths. Literal interpretations restrict our ability to experience the transformative power of scripture. As we embrace the symbolic meaning of the Bible, we unlock its power to guide us toward personal transformation, spiritual awakening, and manifestation.

The stories of the Bible are not historical accounts but powerful metaphors for our own spiritual journeys. By recognising the allegorical nature of these stories, we can move beyond the veil of literalism and tap into the life-changing power of the Bible’s symbolic language. When we do this, we allow the Bible to work within us, awakening the imagination and the spirit, and transforming our lives in the process.
